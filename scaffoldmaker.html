Background

What is
Scaffoldmaker?

The nervous system controls virtually every organ in the body — but mapping precisely how nerves connect to tissue in three dimensions is extraordinarily difficult. The NIH SPARC programme (Stimulating Peripheral Activity to Relieve Conditions) was set up to do exactly that, with the goal of developing new bioelectronic treatments for disease.

A key challenge: researchers extracting tissue specimens need a shared anatomical reference — a 3D model of the organ they can anchor their data to. That's what Scaffoldmaker provides. It's an open-source Python library for generating mathematically precise, species-specific anatomical scaffold models using the finite element method.

My role at Auckland Bioengineering Institute was to develop scaffold models for specific organs — building the geometry, validating the mesh structure, and ensuring the models could support real experimental data registration from international research groups.

01

Build the geometry

Each organ is defined as a parametric surface mesh using the finite element method — mathematically smooth, watertight, and anatomically proportioned for each target species.

02

Register experimental data

Researchers extract tissue specimens and record their 3D coordinates. Those coordinates are mapped onto the scaffold, anchoring real biological data to a consistent anatomical reference.

03

Simulate & visualise

The scaffold becomes a substrate for simulation — including time-varying deformation. The beating heart model, for example, replays subject-specific cardiac motion with registered data overlaid.

Outputs

Models I built

Each scaffold represents months of anatomical research, mesh refinement, and validation against experimental data. These models are publicly accessible via the SPARC portal and used by research groups internationally.

Heart Scaffold — Subject-Specific Beating Model

The heart scaffold was one of the flagship deliverables of the ABI SPARC project. The model captures the full ventricular geometry and is capable of subject-specific deformation — meaning we can replay a patient's or animal subject's actual cardiac motion, and register the precise 3D location of every tissue specimen extracted during an experiment.

This enables researchers to say exactly where in the heart a specimen came from — something previously impossible with flat diagrams or generic anatomy references. Click the simulation above to interact with it directly — rotate, zoom, and watch it beat in real time.

Process

How a scaffold is built

01

Anatomical research

Study anatomy literature and imaging data to understand the target organ's geometry and species variation

02

Mesh design

Define the finite element topology — node layout, element connectivity, and basis functions — in Python

03

Geometry fitting

Tune node positions and derivatives to match anatomical proportions across target species and sex

04

Validation

Compare scaffold against imaging data and experimental feedback from partner research groups

05

Deployment

Merge into the open-source Scaffoldmaker library and publish to the SPARC portal for global use

Significance

Why this work matters

🫀

Enabling bioelectronic medicine

SPARC is a foundational effort to map the body's neural circuitry — a prerequisite for developing precise bioelectronic therapies that stimulate specific nerves to treat chronic disease without pharmaceuticals. These scaffolds are the anatomical reference layer that makes that mapping possible.

📍

Precise data registration

Before scaffolds, researchers had no shared 3D reference for locating where their specimens came from. A scaffold gives every experiment a common coordinate system — dramatically improving reproducibility and cross-study comparison.

🌏

Open science, global reach

All scaffold models are open-source and hosted on the SPARC portal, accessible to research groups worldwide. The models built during this project are still in active use by international collaborators.
